Lecture 2: Approaches to Rurality – Core Framework (Original English)
1. Functional Approach to Rurality
Core: Map + Measure; empirical, quantifiable indicators
Definition: Dominated by extensive land uses (agriculture, forestry); small, lower-order settlements; cohesive landscape-linked identity
Example: OECD classification (predominantly urban <20% rural population; intermediate 20–50%; predominantly rural ≥50%)
Strengths: Descriptive; identifies trends
Weaknesses: Theory-free, value-free; cannot explain spatial change; urban-rural boundaries blurring
2. Political Economy Approaches
Core: Politics of place and power struggles
Key themes: Post-productivist countryside; Differentiated countryside
Four countryside types: preserved; contested; paternalistic; clientelist
New rural conflicts: agricultural decline; environmentalism; new rural space demands; commuting/migration; social exclusion
3. Cultural Turn in Rural Studies
Core: Rurality as a social construct (contested concept)
Focus: Lay discourses; policy discourses; Rural Idyll
Methods: Qualitative; ethnography; discourse/interpretive analysis
Conflict: Rural community sustainability vs. conservation/aesthetic preservation
Relevance: Reveals policy perspectives; explains rural residential appeal
4. A ‘Capitals’ Framework for Rurality
Core: Asset-based approach; interplay and conversion of capitals
Capitals: Economic; Cultural; Social; Natural
Idea: Mutually reinforcing; unsustainable practice erodes capitals; supports wellbeing-focused planning
5. The Global Countryside
Core: Relational understanding of space; globalisation-driven rural transformation
Meta-narrative: Global commodity networks; transnational corporate integration; international labour/FDI; social polarisation; governance rescaling
Emerging rural functions: Playground; resource sink; cultural heritage repository; production base; ecosystem service provider; living space
New types: Urban countryside; Neoliberal countryside; Post-carbon countryside
